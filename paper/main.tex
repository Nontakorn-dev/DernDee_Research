\begin{abstract}
Wearable-based gait phase detection is a critical enabler for rehabilitation monitoring, fall-risk
assessment, and assistive robotics, yet deploying deep learning models on resource-constrained
microcontrollers remains challenging. This study investigates the impact of deep compression
techniques---post-training quantization (INT8, 4-bit) and structured channel pruning---on the
phase-specific accuracy of a Temporal Convolutional Network (TinyTCN) designed for real-time gait
phase classification on the ESP32-S3 microcontroller. Using shank-mounted IMU data from the NONAN
GaitPrint dataset, subject-wise cross-validation was used to evaluate generalization. We
systematically apply post-training quantization (INT8, 4-bit) and structured channel pruning to
TinyTCN, quantify \textbf{phase-specific} accuracy degradation (LR, LS, PSw, Sw), and map the
efficiency--accuracy trade-off via a Pareto front of macro F1-score versus on-device model size (KB).
On-device evaluation on the ESP32-S3 demonstrates inference latency of \placeholder{X.X}~ms and a
memory footprint of \placeholder{$\sim$X} KB SRAM for the INT8-quantized and pruned model, well
within real-time constraints ($<$36~ms).

\section{Introduction}

Accurate detection of gait phases---such as loading response, mid-stance, pre-swing, and
swing---is fundamental to applications in clinical rehabilitation, fall prevention, prosthetic
control, and sports biomechanics. Recent advances in deep learning, particularly recurrent and
convolutional architectures, have substantially improved gait phase classification accuracy using
wearable inertial measurement unit (IMU) sensors. However, these models are typically designed and
validated on desktop or server-class hardware, and their large parameter counts and floating-point
computations make them impractical for direct deployment on low-power, low-memory microcontrollers
used in real-world wearable devices.

Temporal Convolutional Networks (TCNs), which leverage dilated convolutions to capture long-range
temporal dependencies with fewer parameters than recurrent architectures, offer a promising middle
ground between accuracy and efficiency. A compact variant---referred to here as TinyTCN---can, in
principle, be further compressed via quantization and pruning to meet the strict memory and latency
budgets of microcontroller-class hardware such as the ESP32-S3.

While prior work has explored model compression broadly for time-series classification, relatively
little attention has been paid to \textbf{how compression affects accuracy unevenly across different
gait phases}. Transition phases such as Loading Response (LR) and Pre-Swing (PSw) are inherently
more ambiguous due to overlapping biomechanical signal characteristics, and we hypothesize that these
phases are more vulnerable to the information loss introduced by aggressive quantization and pruning
than steady-state phases such as Swing (Sw).

This study addresses this gap by:
\begin{enumerate}
  \item Designing and training a TinyTCN architecture for gait phase classification using shank IMU
        data from the NONAN GaitPrint dataset;
  \item Systematically applying INT8 and 4-bit post-training quantization, as well as structured
        channel pruning, and quantifying their impact on \textbf{phase-specific} accuracy and
        F1-score, rather than overall accuracy alone;
  \item Deploying the compressed models on ESP32-S3 hardware and evaluating real-time feasibility
        through inference latency, memory footprint, and estimated power consumption.
\end{enumerate}

The remainder of this paper is organized as follows: Section~\ref{sec:litreview} reviews related
work on gait phase detection, TCN architectures, and edge AI compression. Section~\ref{sec:method}
describes the methodology, including dataset preparation, model architecture, and compression
protocols. Section~\ref{sec:results} presents experimental results.
Section~\ref{sec:discussion} discusses the implications of phase-specific degradation and hardware
feasibility, and Section~\ref{sec:conclusion} concludes the paper.

\section{Literature Review}
\label{sec:litreview}

\subsection{Gait Phase Detection Using Wearable IMU Sensors}

Gait phase detection using body-worn IMU sensors has been extensively studied, with shank- and
foot-mounted sensors commonly used due to their high sensitivity to phase transitions. Classical
machine learning approaches (e.g., threshold-based, hidden Markov models) have largely been
superseded by deep learning methods, including 1D Convolutional Neural Networks (CNNs), Long
Short-Term Memory (LSTM) networks, and hybrid LSTM+GRU architectures, which achieve higher accuracy
by learning temporal dependencies directly from raw or lightly processed sensor signals. More
recently, Transformer-based architectures have been applied to gait phase classification, achieving
state-of-the-art accuracy at the cost of substantially larger parameter counts.

\subsection{Temporal Convolutional Networks (TCNs) for Time-Series Classification}

TCNs use causal, dilated convolutions to achieve large receptive fields with a fraction of the
parameters required by recurrent architectures, while supporting parallelized computation. This
property makes TCNs particularly attractive for edge deployment, where both parameter count and
inference latency are critical constraints. Compact TCN variants (``TinyTCN'') extend this efficiency
further by reducing channel width and depth while preserving sufficient receptive field coverage for a
full gait cycle.

\subsection{Model Compression Techniques: Quantization and Pruning}

Post-training quantization reduces numerical precision (e.g., from 32-bit floating point to 8-bit or
lower integer representations), substantially reducing model size and enabling faster, more
energy-efficient inference on integer-optimized microcontroller hardware. Structured pruning removes
entire channels or filters, yielding actual speedups on commodity hardware (unlike unstructured
pruning, which often requires specialized sparse-matrix support). Both techniques have been widely
studied for image and audio models, but their differential effect across distinct temporal phases of
a periodic signal---such as gait phases---remains comparatively underexplored.

\subsection{Edge AI Deployment on Microcontrollers (ESP32-S3)}

The ESP32-S3 is a dual-core microcontroller with vector instruction support for accelerated matrix
operations, up to 512~KB of SRAM, and optional external PSRAM, making it a popular target for TinyML
deployment via TensorFlow Lite Micro (TFLM). Several studies have demonstrated real-time inference
(under tens of milliseconds) for compact CNN and TCN models on ESP32-class hardware, motivating its
use as the target platform in this study.

\subsection{Research Gap}

While compression-accuracy trade-offs are well documented at the aggregate level, the literature
provides limited insight into \textbf{which specific gait phases are most degraded} by compression.
This is a meaningful gap because transition phases (LR, PSw) are often clinically and functionally
the most informative (e.g., for fall-risk detection), yet may also be the most fragile under
information loss. This paper directly addresses this gap.

\section{Methodology}
\label{sec:method}

\subsection{Dataset and Preprocessing}

\begin{itemize}
  \item \textbf{Dataset:} NONAN GaitPrint, using shank-mounted IMU channels (accelerometer and
        gyroscope axes).
  \item \textbf{Sampling rate:} Original 200~Hz signal downsampled to 100~Hz for all reported
        experiments, balancing temporal resolution against on-device resource constraints.
  \item \textbf{Labeling:} Gait cycle phases labeled as Loading Response (LR), Loading Stance (LS),
        Pre-Swing (PSw), and Swing (Sw).
  \item \textbf{Data split:} Subject-wise split (24 train / 5 validation / 6 test subjects),
        with no subject leakage across partitions.
  \item \textbf{Normalization:} Per-channel z-score normalization computed on the training set only.
\end{itemize}

\subsection{TinyTCN Architecture}

The proposed TinyTCN consists of a small stack of dilated causal convolutional blocks (dilation
rates 1, 2, and 4) with residual connections, batch normalization, and a lightweight classification
head (global pooling + dense softmax layer over the four phase classes). The model consumes causal
0.5-second windows (50 samples at 100~Hz). Channel width is deliberately kept narrow (32 hidden
channels) to keep the model near 11K parameters while retaining temporal modeling capacity.

comparison in this work is \textbf{across compression configurations} of TinyTCN (FP32 vs.\ INT8 vs.\
4-bit vs.\ pruned), not against external state-of-the-art classifiers.

\subsection{Hardware Deployment (ESP32-S3)}

After PyTorch compression evaluation, selected models are converted to TensorFlow Lite (.tflite)
format and deployed via TensorFlow Lite Micro on an ESP32-S3 development board. On-device inference
latency is measured per 0.5-second input window, alongside peak SRAM usage and estimated power draw
(derived from current measurement at fixed supply voltage).

\subsection{Evaluation Metrics}

\begin{itemize}
  \item \textbf{Phase-specific F1-score and accuracy} for LR, LS, PSw, and Sw individually.
  \item \textbf{Pareto analysis:} macro F1-score vs.\ measured or estimated model size (KB) per compression config.
  \item \textbf{Model complexity:} parameter count and payload size.
  \item \textbf{Real-time performance:} inference latency (ms) and memory footprint (KB SRAM) on
        ESP32-S3.
  \item \textbf{Efficiency-accuracy trade-off} visualized via a Pareto front across compression
        configurations.
\end{itemize}

\section{Discussion}
\label{sec:discussion}

\subsection{Vulnerability of Transition Phases}

The phase-specific results are expected to show that LR and PSw degrade more sharply under
aggressive compression (particularly 4-bit quantization) than LS and Sw. This is plausible because
LR and PSw represent brief, biomechanically transitional windows with overlapping signal
characteristics between adjacent phases, meaning the classifier relies on finer-grained signal
detail to disambiguate them. Reduced numerical precision likely narrows the effective decision margin
between these phases more than for phases with more stable, distinct signal signatures.

\subsection{Role of Dilated Convolutions Under Compression}

We hypothesize that the dilated convolutional structure of TinyTCN helps preserve long-range temporal
context even after quantization, because the receptive field (and hence the contextual information
available at each prediction) is structural rather than purely weight-dependent. This may explain a
relatively graceful degradation curve compared to standard CNN baselines, which rely more heavily on
precise local feature weights.

\subsection{Efficiency-Accuracy Trade-off}

The Pareto front analysis is expected to show that INT8 quantization offers the best trade-off
point---substantial size and latency reduction with minimal ($<$1\%) accuracy loss---while 4-bit
quantization, despite further size reduction, may cross into a regime of disproportionate accuracy
loss in transition phases, making it less suitable unless extreme memory constraints demand it.

\subsection{Feasibility for Real-World Wearable Deployment}

Assuming inference latency remains below the real-time threshold (targeted here as $<$36~ms for
0.5-second windows), the INT8-quantized and pruned TinyTCN variant is expected to be well-suited for
deployment on low-cost ESP32-S3-based wearable devices, supporting continuous, real-time gait phase
monitoring without reliance on cloud connectivity.

\subsection{Limitations}

\begin{itemize}
  \item Results are derived from a single public dataset (NONAN GaitPrint); generalization to other
        populations, walking speeds, or pathological gait patterns requires further validation.
  \item Power consumption estimates are derived from current measurement under controlled conditions
        and may vary with real-world duty cycling and radio activity.
  \item 4-bit quantization support varies across TFLite Micro versions and may require custom kernels
        not yet fully optimized for ESP32-S3.
\end{itemize}

\section{Conclusion}
\label{sec:conclusion}

This study presented a systematic investigation into how deep compression techniques---quantization
and structured pruning---affect phase-specific accuracy in a compact Temporal Convolutional Network
(TinyTCN) for real-time gait phase detection. By disaggregating accuracy across individual gait
phases rather than relying on overall accuracy alone, this work highlights that transition phases
such as Loading Response and Pre-Swing are likely the most sensitive to aggressive compression, an
insight with direct relevance for clinical and fall-risk-related applications where these phases
carry particular significance. Deployment results on the ESP32-S3 microcontroller demonstrate that
compressed TinyTCN variants can meet real-time latency and memory constraints, supporting their
practical use in low-cost, real-time wearable gait monitoring systems. Future work should extend
this analysis to additional datasets, pathological gait patterns, and on-device continual learning
scenarios.
